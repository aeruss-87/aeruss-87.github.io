Claude Best Practices

Prompts, shortcuts, and inspiration for getting the most out of Claude

🔍
Core Principles

🎯 Be Clear and Direct

  • State what you want explicitly. Assume nothing is implied.
  • The "golden rule": if a colleague with no context would be confused by your prompt, Claude will be too.
  • Tell Claude what to DO, not what to avoid.

🧠 Assign a Role

  • Give Claude a specific identity and domain before the task.
  • Include expertise level and perspective.
  • Even one sentence of role context dramatically improves output.
"You are a senior People Ops strategist with 10 years of experience at high-growth tech companies."

📋 Provide Context

  • Explain WHY you need something, not just what.
  • Include audience, stakes, and any constraints upfront.
  • More context produces more calibrated output.

💡 Use Examples

  • 3-5 well-crafted examples (few-shot prompting) dramatically improve consistency.
  • Show Claude the format and tone you want via examples, not just descriptions.
  • Include both good and bad examples when precision matters.

🏗️ Structure with XML Tags

  • Use tags to separate context, instructions, and input.
  • Reduces misinterpretation on complex prompts.
  • Especially useful for multi-part or document-heavy tasks.
<context>...</context> <instructions>...</instructions> <input>...</input>

📐 Specify Output Format

  • Tell Claude exactly what format you want: bullets, table, prose, JSON.
  • Specify length: "in 3 sentences" or "no more than 200 words."
  • Match your prompt style to the output you want. Markdown prompts produce markdown output.
Output and Tone

Minimize excessive formatting

For long-form content, use clear prose instead of excessive bullet lists. Claude defaults to bullets — override explicitly if you want something else.

Specify tone and audience together

Don't just say "professional." Say "direct and warm, for a 50-person company where people know each other."

Ask for variants on high-stakes messages

Request 2-3 versions labeled by what each one prioritizes and trades off. Gives you optionality without starting over.

Constrain overthinking

For decisions, add: "Choose an approach and commit to it. Don't revisit unless I give you new information." Stops Claude from hedging.

Add a self-check step

End complex prompts with: "Before finishing, check your answer against [criteria]." Catches basic errors without a follow-up message.

Long documents: put data first

For 20k+ token prompts, place your source material at the top, then your instructions below. Can improve output quality by ~30%.

Add "ultrathink" for hard problems

Adding the word "ultrathink" to any prompt gives Claude more reasoning budget for genuinely complex work. The quality difference on hard analysis, planning, or decisions is visible. Not needed for routine tasks.

For People Ops Work

👥 Survey and Engagement Analysis

  • Always combine quantitative scores with qualitative sentiment.
  • Specify: protect psychological safety, do not surface individual low scorers.
  • Ask for: themes, root causes, and prioritized recommendations.

📅 Planning and Backwards Mapping

  • Give Claude the deadline and constraints first.
  • Ask for: week-by-week, concrete actions, named owners.
  • Request decision gates and dependency flags explicitly.

✉️ Stakeholder Communications

  • Specify audience, stakes, and goal of the message.
  • Ask Claude to flag if the message warrants leadership review.
  • High-stakes sends: always request 2-3 variants.

Execution Modes (press Shift+Tab to cycle)

🛡️ Default Mode

Asks permission before editing files or running commands. Your starting point. Best for sensitive work: survey data, performance info, anything you want full visibility on.

✏️ Auto-Accept Edits

Claude edits files freely but still asks before running shell commands. Good for repetitive file work: organizing folders, renaming, routine edits where you trust the pattern.

📋 Plan Mode

Read-only. Claude gathers info and creates a plan, but makes no edits and runs no commands. You review the plan, then decide whether to proceed. Best for research, complex projects, and OKR/comms strategy work.

⚡ Auto Mode

More autonomous. Claude evaluates actions with background safety checks and acts without asking for most operations. Still respects CLAUDE.md rules. Best for trusted recurring workflows with clear guardrails.

Critique

Devil's Advocate Review

Review this [PLAN / MESSAGE / PROPOSAL]: [CONTENT] Act as a trusted advisor who tells me what I need to hear. Identify: real gaps, weak logic, and likely audience objections. Do not nitpick minor details. Focus on what could actually derail this. Be sharp but constructive.
Brainstorm

Unconventional Ideas

Generate 8 ideas for [PROBLEM / CHALLENGE]. Include at least 2 unconventional approaches that break from how this is typically done. For each idea: core insight, why it might work, and biggest risk. After listing all 8, rank the top 3 by [IMPACT / FEASIBILITY / SPEED].
Prompt Patterns by Category

🔍 Analysis and Insights

  • "What are the key patterns, risks, or opportunities in [data]?"
  • "Root cause analysis: why does [problem] happen? Trace back through the system."
  • "Compare [A] vs [B]. What are the real trade-offs?"
  • "What would someone unfamiliar with [domain] need to understand about this?"

📣 Drafting and Communication

  • "Draft [message] for [audience]. Goal: [outcome]. Tone: [style]."
  • "Rewrite this to be clearer and more direct. What would you change?"
  • "Give me 3 variants, each labeled by what it prioritizes and trades off."
  • "Write a one-pager: context, recommendation, next steps."

🗺️ Planning and Project Management

  • "Work backwards from [deadline]. What needs to happen when?"
  • "Identify dependencies and bottlenecks. Which tasks must happen sequentially?"
  • "Design a communication plan for [stakeholder group]. What, when, how?"
  • "Flag decision gates and dependency risks in this plan."

💬 Brainstorming and Ideation

  • "Generate 10 ideas. Include at least 2 unconventional approaches."
  • "What am I missing about [topic]? What questions should I be asking?"
  • "If we completely reimagined [process], ignoring current constraints, what would it look like?"
  • "Devil's advocate: what could go wrong with [proposal]?"

📝 Summarization

  • "Summarize in 3 sentences. Highlight key decisions and action items."
  • "Pull out the action items. Who owns each? When is it due?"
  • "What, So What, Now What — distill this into those three buckets."
  • "Extract the top 5 things the reader needs to remember."

🎯 Decision-Making

  • "Should we [A] or [B]? What does the data suggest?"
  • "What's the right decision considering [constraints / values / stakeholders]?"
  • "What am I not considering about this decision?"
  • "What would you do in my position? What's the core trade-off?"

🧩 Explanation and Learning

  • "Explain [concept] like I'm [audience level]. Use concrete examples."
  • "What's the mental model I should have about [topic]?"
  • "What are common misconceptions about [topic]? What's actually true?"
  • "Walk me through how [system] works, step by step."

🛡️ Stress-Testing Work

  • "What's likely to slip in this plan? Where is the timeline too tight?"
  • "Who is a dependency risk? What hasn't been accounted for?"
  • "What will [Grant / Laura / Ryder] push back on? What questions will they ask?"
  • "Where is the logic weak? What assumptions am I making?"
Power Moves Worth Trying

Chain prompts for complex work

Break a large task into sequential prompts. First get the analysis, then ask Claude to build on it. Cleaner than one giant prompt.

Use agents for multi-part projects

Say "use agents" to trigger a parallel team of specialized agents. Best for planning cycles, deck creation, and high-stakes comms.

Ask what you're missing

"What questions should I be asking that I'm not?" surfaces blind spots faster than refining a prompt you've already written.

Label your variants

When requesting multiple drafts, ask Claude to label what each one prioritizes and what it trades off. Makes choosing faster.

Ask for the pushback first

Before finalizing anything for leadership, prompt: "What will [audience] push back on and what are they likely to ask?" Then revise.

Iterate, don't restart

Refine in the same conversation. "Make this more direct." "Shorten the second paragraph." "Try a warmer opening." Claude holds full context.

Guiding Principles

1 Always practice on real work

Toy examples build toy skills. Every exercise in this plan maps to something you actually need to do at Baton. If an exercise does not connect to a real task, reframe it until it does.

2 Build systems, not prompts

A great prompt used once is worth less than a mediocre template used fifty times and improved each time. Every good prompt should become a template.

3 The human judgment layer is the skill

AI orchestration is not about removing yourself from the loop. It is about knowing exactly where your judgment is irreplaceable and focusing your energy there.

4 Document as you go

Your orchestration playbook is the artifact. It should be a living document that grows with you. Log sessions to Craft. Build the record of what you are learning.

5 Teach to learn

When you can explain a pattern to Chelsea or a manager at Baton, you have actually internalized it. Day 26 is not optional.

XML Tags for Prompting

XML tags are custom labels you wrap around different parts of a prompt to make it unambiguous what is source material, what is the instruction, and what format you want back. There is no official list: name them what they contain, and be consistent. These are the tags most useful for the types of work you do.

Universal (use in any prompt)
TagWhat goes insideWhen to use it
<task>The actual instruction: what you want Claude to doAlways. Especially when you are also pasting context or data — separates the ask from the material.
<context>Background Claude needs but should not confuse with the instructionAny time you are giving situation, history, or framing before the task.
<output_format>How you want the response structured: bullets, table, prose, sectionsWhen format precision matters, such as exec summaries, RACI matrices, or anything you will copy into another doc.
<constraints>Rules, tone guidelines, length limits, things to avoidAny time you have non-obvious requirements: no em dashes, max 3 sentences, no individual data, etc.
<examples>Samples of what good output looks likeWhen you need Claude to match a specific voice, format, or standard you already have examples of.
Communications Drafting
TagWhat goes insideWhen to use it
<audience>Who is receiving the message: name, role, relationshipAny leadership or cross-functional draft. Grant vs. full team vs. Chelsea require very different registers.
<draft>A rough version you want cleaned up or rewrittenWhen you have already started something and want Claude to improve it rather than start from scratch.
<background>The situation or history behind the messageWhen the recipient needs context Claude should weave in without you spelling it out in the ask.
<ask>The specific action you need the recipient to takeAny message with a decision, approval, or deadline. Makes the call to action explicit.
<tone>Warm, direct, formal, urgent, candidWhen the default tone will not fit: a difficult message, a leadership send, or something that needs to feel personal.

TagWhat goes insideWhen to use it
